Measurements Of Dispersion

In statistics, dispersion is the extent to which a distribution is stretched or squeezed. Common examples of measures of statistical dispersion are the variance, standard deviation, and interquartile range. For instance, when the variance of data in a set is large, the data is widely scattered.

The study of spread of data is continusly used throghtout computer science. One of the most popular uses in current times is data analysis done by large companies.

Range and Quartiles:

The range of a set of data is very simple to find. range - highest value- lowest value Quartiles-Q1, Q2, Q3, spil the data into four parts. 25% of the observations should have a vlaue of less than the lower quartile, Q1, 50% of the observations should be below the median,Q2 and 75% of the observations should be below the upper quartile, Q3.

Method used for discrete data

  • To calculate the lower quartile, Q1, divide n by 4. When n⁄4 is a whole number find the mid-point of the corrresponding term and the term above. When n⁄4 is not a whole number round the number up and pick the corresponding term.

  • To calculate the upper quartile, Q3, divide n by 4 and multiply by 3. When 3n⁄4 is a whole number find the mid-point of the corrresponding term and the term above. When 3n⁄4 is not a whole number round the number up and pick the corresponding term.

Method used for continous data

  • To calculate the lower quartile, Q1, divide n by 4 and use interpolation to find the corresponding value.

  • To calculate the upper quartile, Q3, divide n by 4 and multiply by 3 and use interpolation to find the corresponding value.

Interquartile range = upper quartile - lower quartile

Standard Deviation and Variance:

The deviaiton of an observation x from the mean is given by x - x̄, so one way of measuring the total dispersion (spread) of a set of data is to use variance.

The sqaure root of the variance is called the standard deviaiton.
