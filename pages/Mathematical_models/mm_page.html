Mathematical Models

A mathematical model is a simplification of a real-world situation. It can be used to make predictions and forecasts about real-world situations. This helps solve and improve the understanding of real-world situations by analysing the results and the model.

An example of mathematical model being used in computer science is scheduling done by operating systems.

Mathematical models are useful because: They are quick and easy to produce They are easy to understand Variables can be readily changed making them versatile They are used to make predictions They can help provide control

However they must be treated with caution as they tend to work in certain scenarios only and simplification of problems often leads to omission of variables

The process of designing a model generally involves seven stages, outlined below.

  1. Stage 1: The recognition of a real-world problem
  2. Stage 2: A mathematical model is devised
  3. Stage 3: Model used to make predictions about the behaviour of the real-world problem
  4. Stage 4: Experimental data are collected from the real world
  5. Stage 5: Comparisons are made against the devised model
  6. Stage 6: Statistical concepts are used to test how well the model describes the real-world problem
  7. Stage 7: Model is refined

A scientist is investigating the population of owls and notices that the population varies year to year. Give a summary of the stages that are needed to create a mathematical model for this population variation. 1 Some assumptions need to be made to ensure the model is manageable. Birth and death rates of owls should be included, but food supply and environment changes should not. 2 Plan a mathematical model which will include diagrams. 3 Use this model to predict the population of the owls over a period of years. 4 Include and collect fresh data that match the conditions of the predicted values. You may also use historical data from the previous years. 5 Analyse the data using techniques you will meet in this course to compare the predicted data with the experimental data. 6 Use statistical tests that will provide an objective means of deciding if the differences between the model's predictions and experimental data are within acceptable limits. If the predicted values do not match the experimental data closely enough, then the model can be refined. This will involve repeating and refining steps 2-6. This model is then constantly refined making the model more and more accurate.
